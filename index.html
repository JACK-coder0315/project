Project 2: Persuasive or Misleading Visualizations?

Claim A: NYPD’s Accountability System Fails — Most Complaints Are Dismissed

Chart A: Over 70% of NYPD complaints were not substantiated, suggesting systemic failure in police accountability.

This visualization supports the claim that the NYPD’s internal complaint handling system is ineffective. By using a red bar to emphasize the number of unsubstantiated complaints (23,749), it guides the viewer’s emotional reaction toward concern. The straightforward bar chart makes the imbalance visually stark and easily interpretable.

Claim B: Some NYPD Precincts Show High Substantiation Rates

Chart B: In some NYPD precincts, more than 70–80% of complaints are substantiated, suggesting localized accountability.

This visualization argues against the claim by highlighting that not all complaints are dismissed. In several precincts, the substantiation rate exceeds 70%, suggesting that in some areas, accountability mechanisms are working. The green color palette reinforces this positive narrative.

Reflection

While both visualizations are based on the same dataset, the design choices — color, aggregation, and framing — guide viewer perception. Chart A aggregates all complaints to emphasize overall failure, while Chart B disaggregates by location to show potential progress. Together, they demonstrate that data visualization is not purely objective: it carries rhetorical power depending on how the story is framed. For ethical visualization, transparency in intent, labeling, and context is key.
